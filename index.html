01

Exam structure & scoring

Part A — Multiple choice / T&F
~40 pts 4–8 MCQ questions (5–8 pts each) plus True/False (12 pts). No partial credit. Fast and high-stakes.
Part B — Prove or disprove
~32 pts 4 statements, 8 pts each. Must prove or find a concrete counterexample. Partial credit possible.
Part C — Open questions
~40 pts Q11: theorem proof + application (~22 pts). Q12: calculation + integral (~18 pts).
Time allocation

Spend ~25 min on Part A, ~30 min on Part B, ~35 min on Part C. Never get stuck on one MCQ for more than 3 minutes — mark your best guess and move on. Always attempt Q12 (the integral/approximation question) — it has clear sub-parts and is the most predictable.

02

MCQ question types decoded

The MCQ section is heavily pattern-based. Every exam contains these recurring question archetypes:

Every exam
Piecewise / exotic function
A function like e^{-1/x³} or sin(1/x)·x is given. Test: injectivity, integrability, differentiability, even/odd, asymptotes. Use limits from both sides and Taylor expansions around 0.
Every exam
Two improper integrals
Four options: both converge, neither, I only, II only. Identify the problematic point(s), do comparison test near that point using known rates: polynomials vs. log vs. sin/cos-derived singularities.
Every exam
Two series: converge or not?
Same 4-option structure. Apply asymptotic equivalence: replace complicated terms with their dominant asymptotic (e.g. arctan(1/n) ~ 1/n, 1-cos(1/n) ~ 1/(2n²)) then use p-series or comparison.
Every exam
"Mark the correct/incorrect statement"
Tests conceptual traps: convexity, local vs. global extrema, differentiability vs. continuity, inflection points. Know your standard counterexamples cold.
Most exams
Number of roots of an equation
Use IVT to show existence, then differentiation to show monotonicity for uniqueness. Often: show f(0) and f(∞) have opposite signs, then show f′ has constant sign.
Most exams
Power series interval of convergence
Find radius R via ratio/root test. Then check endpoints separately using alternating series test, p-series, or divergence test. Know: if a_n → L ≠ 0, radius = 1.
Key counterexamples to memorize

• f(x) = x sin(1/x), f(0)=0: continuous but not differentiable at 0 in a nice way
• a_n = sin(n)/n: converges (squeeze) even though it's not monotone
• f(x) = x²: inflection point ≠ local extremum (no inflection). But f(x) = x³ has both
• f(x) = |x|: continuous everywhere, not differentiable at 0, and |f| can be differentiable even when f isn't

03

Limits & L'Hôpital

Standard limit types & strategies

0/0 or ∞/∞ forms
L'Hôpital directly. But first try algebraic simplification or known equivalents — it's often faster. Watch: L'Hôpital fails if the limit of f'/g' doesn't exist (e.g. sin/x as x→∞ oscillates).
1^∞ form — (1+something small)^(large)
Rewrite as e^(large · ln(1+small)), then use ln(1+u) ≈ u for small u. Example: lim (1+x)^(1/x) as x→0 or x→∞. The answer e^a where a = lim (large)·(small).
∞/∞ with trig inside (e.g. x/(x−sin x))
Don't use L'Hôpital repeatedly — use Taylor. sin x = x − x³/6 + … so x − sin x = x³/6. Then the ratio becomes simple.
Limit involving integral with variable bounds
Use Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to differentiate top/bottom (this is L'Hôpital applied to an integral quotient). The exam has this often in MCQ Q7 style: lim [∫₀^{x²} f(t)dt] / x·sin²x.
Limit of sequence (discrete): √n·power sums
The classic: lim (1^p + 2^p + … + n^p)^(1/n). Use Riemann sum interpretation or Cesàro–Stolz. Appears in midterms frequently.
Asymptotic equivalents — memorize these

As u → 0: sin u ~ u, tan u ~ u, 1-cos u ~ u²/2, ln(1+u) ~ u, e^u-1 ~ u, arctan u ~ u, arcsin u ~ u, (1+u)^α - 1 ~ αu

04

Sequences

Proving convergence / divergence

Recursively defined sequences
Very common in Part B and old-style Part C. Strategy: (1) Show monotone (by induction or a_{n+1} - a_n). (2) Show bounded. (3) Let L = limit of both sides of recurrence, solve for L. Common pitfall: multiple fixed points — check which one the sequence actually approaches.
Sequence defined by sum
If a_n = Σ_{k=1}^n b_k then convergence of the sequence ↔ convergence of the series Σb_k. Also: if Σ a_k = 5 is given, then a_n → 0 (necessary condition for series convergence).
The squeeze theorem checklist

1. Find lower and upper bounds that squeeze to the same limit. 2. Bounds must be explicit and provable — not hand-waving. 3. For trig sequences: use -1 ≤ sin/cos ≤ 1 to eliminate oscillation. Example: lim n·sin(1/n) = 1 via sin(1/n)/(1/n) → 1.

Subsequences & accumulation points

Know: if every subsequence of {a_n} that converges, converges to the same limit L, then a_n → L. Contrapositive: if you find two subsequences converging to different values, the sequence diverges.

05

Series & convergence

Series questions appear in both MCQ and Part B every single exam. Master the decision tree below.

Convergence test decision tree

  1. Divergence test first: If a_n ↛ 0, the series diverges immediately. Always check.
  2. Recognize standard forms: geometric (|r| < 1), p-series (Σ1/n^p converges iff p > 1), telescoping.
  3. Comparison / limit comparison: Find the dominant asymptotic of a_n. Replace complicated expressions: arctan(1/n) ~ 1/n, 1-cos(1/n) ~ 1/(2n²), ln(n+1)-ln(n) ~ 1/n, (e^{1/n}-1) ~ 1/n. Then compare to a p-series.
  4. Ratio test: Best for factorials and exponentials ((2n)!/n!, n^n/n!). If L=1, test is inconclusive.
  5. Root test: Best when the whole term is raised to the n-th power ((f(n))^n).
  6. Alternating series (Leibniz): Σ(-1)^n b_n converges if b_n is decreasing and b_n → 0. Note: convergence may be conditional (not absolute).
  7. Integral test: Use when a_n = f(n) for a nice continuous decreasing function. Rare on this exam.
Exam trap: comparing absolute vs. conditional convergence

The MCQ often asks: if Σa_n converges, what can you say about Σa_n², Σ|a_n|/√n, etc.? Key fact: convergence of Σa_n alone tells you very little about other series — you need absolute convergence for most manipulations. The answer to "does convergence of Σa_n imply Σa_n² converges?" is no in general, but yes if convergent absolutely.

Evaluating series sums (not just convergence)

Several exams ask you to evaluate a sum, not just classify it. Techniques used:

  • Recognize as Taylor series: Match to e^x, sin x, cos x, ln(1+x), arctan x at a specific x value.
  • Differentiate or integrate a known power series term-by-term to get the form you need.
  • Telescoping: Write a_n = f(n) - f(n+1) and the sum collapses to f(1) - lim f(n).
06

Power series & Taylor

Finding the interval of convergence

  1. Use ratio test to find R: R = lim |a_n / a_{n+1}| or R = 1/lim |a_n|^{1/n}.
  2. Check the two endpoints x = ±R separately. At endpoints, ratio test gives L=1 (inconclusive), so use other tests.
  3. If the series is Σa_n(x-c)^n, shift appropriately: the center is c, radius is R, so interval is (c-R, c+R) with possible endpoint inclusion.
Shifted power series trick

If the interval of convergence of Σa_n x^n is [-2, 2), then Σa_n (x²+1)^n converges when |x²+1| < 2 and possibly at endpoints. Solve: -2 ≤ x²+1 < 2 → -1 < x² < 1 → -1 < x < 1. Watch out: this appears as a Part B "prove or disprove" question.

Taylor approximation for numerical calculation

Q12(a) or Q7(b) every single exam: compute √something or ln(something) or ∛something with given accuracy. The strategy is always the same:

  1. Identify which standard Taylor series to use: (1+u)^α for roots, ln(1+u) for logarithms.
  2. Write the target as f(x₀ + h) where x₀ is a nice point and h is small. Example: √18 = √(16·(1+1/8)) = 4·(1+1/8)^{1/2}.
  3. Expand using binomial series: (1+u)^α = 1 + αu + α(α-1)u²/2! + …
  4. Find how many terms are needed: the series for roots and logs is alternating after the first term, so the error is bounded by the first omitted term. Keep terms until the first omitted term is smaller than the required accuracy.
  5. State explicitly: "Since this is an alternating series with decreasing terms, the error is less than the first omitted term = [value] < [required accuracy]."
— Key Taylor series at x=0 —
e^x = 1 + x + x²/2! + x³/3! + … (all x)
sin x = x − x³/6 + x⁵/120 − … (all x)
cos x = 1 − x²/2 + x⁴/24 − … (all x)
ln(1+x) = x − x²/2 + x³/3 − … (|x| ≤ 1, x≠-1)
(1+x)^α = 1 + αx + α(α-1)x²/2! + … (|x| < 1)
arctan x = x − x³/3 + x⁵/5 − … (|x| ≤ 1)
1/(1−x) = 1 + x + x² + x³ + … (|x| < 1)
07

Derivatives & function analysis

The big conceptual questions (MCQ Part A, Q4)

Differentiability vs. continuity
Differentiable ⟹ continuous (always). Continuous ⇏ differentiable (|x| at 0).
f² differentiable ⇏ f differentiable. But f differentiable ⟹ f² differentiable.
|f| differentiable ⇏ f differentiable (classic trap).
Monotonicity & derivatives
f′ > 0 on (a,b) ⟹ strictly increasing. But f′ ≥ 0 alone is NOT enough (e.g. x³ at 0). If f′ > 0 everywhere except finitely many points, still strictly increasing.
Convexity & inflection
f′′ > 0 ⟹ convex (not the converse). Inflection point: f′′ changes sign (not just f′′=0). A convex function on all of ℝ with a maximum must be constant. An inflection point CAN be a local extremum only if differentiability fails there.
Inverse function differentiability
If f(x₀) = y₀ and f′(x₀) ≠ 0, then (f⁻¹)′(y₀) = 1/f′(x₀). Tangent line to f⁻¹ at y₀: use this formula. Also: if T₂(x) = f(0) + f′(0)x + … is given, extract f(0) and f′(0) directly.

Function analysis: FTC + differentiation of integrals

Derivative of ∫_{g(x)}^{h(x)} f(t)dt

= f(h(x))·h′(x) − f(g(x))·g′(x). Used constantly in limits via L'Hôpital on integral quotients, and in proving a function is increasing/decreasing (by showing the derivative is positive/negative).

08

Integrals & techniques

Choosing the technique — Q12(b) every exam

∫ (linear)/(quadratic) dx — partial fractions or completing the square
Split numerator: write Ax+B = A(x+b) + C so one part is the derivative of the denominator (→ ln) and one is a pure constant (→ arctan). This exact form (∫(px+q)/(x²+bx+c)dx) appears in 2018A, 2019A, 2022A exams.
Integration by parts: ∫u dv = uv − ∫v du
Use for x·trig, x·e^x, ln(x), x·arctan(x). For the last one (appears on 2019B): let u=arctan(x), dv=x dx. The 2018B exam has a tricky chain with F(x)=∫f(t)dt — differentiate to find h′(x) via chain rule.
Substitution with nested compositions
When you see ∫e^{sin x} cos(sin x) cos x dx (appears on 2020A!): let u = sin x, then du = cos x dx, so it becomes ∫e^u cos(u) du, then parts again.
Trigonometric substitution for √(a²−x²)
Let x = a sin θ. Used for arc length integrals and in ∫x²/√(4−x²)dx style problems. After substitution, use double-angle formulas to simplify.
Improper integrals — convergence analysis
At a singularity x=a: near a, compare f(x) ~ C/(x-a)^p. Converges iff p < 1. At ∞: compare to 1/x^p. Converges iff p > 1. For sin(x-a)/(x-a)² type: near a, sin(x-a)/(x-a)² ~ 1/(x-a) → diverges (like p=1).

Definite integral computation tricks

  • Symmetry: If f is odd and integration is over [-a, a], the integral is 0. Always check before computing.
  • Substitution in definite integral: Update the limits! This is a common careless error.
  • FTC with parameter: If F(x) = ∫_a^{g(x)} f(t)dt, then F′(x) = f(g(x))·g′(x). Use this to prove monotonicity of F.
09

Proof questions (Parts B & C)

Part B is 4 "prove or disprove" statements. Part C Q11 is a theorem proof + application. These have the highest variance in student scores.

Part C — Theorem proofs (you MUST know these)

The proof question rotates between these theorems — one appears every exam:

Appears every year
Rolle's Theorem
f continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), f(a)=f(b) ⟹ ∃c∈(a,b): f′(c)=0. Proof: if f is constant, done. Otherwise by EVT f attains max or min in interior → Fermat gives f′=0 there.
Appears every year
Lagrange / MVT
Apply Rolle to the auxiliary function g(x) = f(x) − f(a) − [(f(b)-f(a))/(b-a)]·(x-a). Verify g(a)=g(b)=0, apply Rolle to g, get a c where g′(c)=0, which gives the MVT conclusion.
Some years
Fermat's Theorem
If x₀ is a local extremum and f is differentiable at x₀, then f′(x₀)=0. Proof: consider the limit definition of f′ from left and right, use the sign of f(x)-f(x₀) to conclude the limit must be both ≥0 and ≤0.
After proving the theorem — the application (10–12 pts)

This is where you apply the theorem you just proved. Common patterns: (1) Use MVT to bound f(b)-f(a) given f′ ≤ g(x). (2) Show an equation has exactly N solutions via IVT (existence) + strict monotonicity (uniqueness). (3) Prove some limit identity using f(x+h)-f(x) → 0 via MVT applied to a specific interval.

Part B — Prove or disprove: systematic approach

  1. Try to disprove first. Think of the simplest possible counterexample. Most false statements can be disproved with a_n = (-1)^n, f(x) = |x|, constant functions, etc.
  2. If you can't find a counterexample in 2 minutes, it's probably true. Start building a proof using the hypothesis.
  3. For implications about limits: use ε-N definitions only if the statement is subtle. Most can be proven using limit arithmetic rules.
  4. Write the counterexample explicitly. State: "Let [construct]. Then [verify hypothesis]. But [show conclusion fails]. ∎"
High-frequency Part B archetypes

• "f(x)/x → 1 ⟹ f(x)−x → 0": FALSE — counterexample: f(x)=x+c for any c≠0.
• "if a_{n+1}−a_n → 0 then {a_n} converges": FALSE — counterexample: a_n = ln(n).
• "if 0 ≤ a_n−a_{n-1} ≤ 1/n² then {a_n} converges": TRUE — a_n is increasing and bounded above (telescoping, Σ1/n² converges).
• "if Σan·bn and Σan²·bn² converge then bn converges": FALSE in general.

Formula sheet — what to add

Your sheet already has: trig identities, log/ln rules, double angle, limit hierarchy, L'Hôpital, derivatives, integrals, integration techniques, Taylor expansion, convergence tests, power series radius. Here is what's missing or under-specified based on the exams:

Asymptotic equivalents (u→0)
Add a small table: sin u ~ u, 1−cos u ~ u²/2, tan u ~ u, e^u−1 ~ u, ln(1+u) ~ u, arctan u ~ u, arcsin u ~ u, (1+u)^α−1 ~ αu. Crucial for series comparison and limits — used on virtually every exam.
Binomial series
(1+x)^α = Σ_{n=0}^∞ C(α,n) x^n where C(α,n) = α(α-1)···(α-n+1)/n! for |x| < 1. Write out the first 3 terms explicitly: 1 + αx + α(α-1)x²/2! + α(α-1)(α-2)x³/3! + …. This is used for every numerical approximation question.
Inverse function derivative
(f⁻¹)′(y₀) = 1 / f′(f⁻¹(y₀)). Appears in MCQ (tangent line to inverse function). If T₂(x) = a + bx + cx² is Maclaurin of f, then f(0)=a, f′(0)=b.
MVT / Lagrange statement
Write it precisely: "If f is continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b), then ∃c∈(a,b) such that f(b)−f(a) = f′(c)·(b−a)." You will need to state this verbatim in Part C.
Rolle's theorem statement
"If f is continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), and f(a)=f(b), then ∃c∈(a,b) such that f′(c)=0." Also write the MVT auxiliary function: g(x) = f(x) − f(a) − [(f(b)−f(a))/(b−a)]·(x−a).
Improper integral convergence rates
Near singularity at x=a: ∫1/(x−a)^p dx converges iff p < 1. At ∞: ∫_{1}^∞ 1/x^p dx converges iff p > 1. Add ∫_1^∞ 1/(x·(ln x)^p)dx converges iff p > 1 (log-p test — appears in series questions too).
Arc length formula
L = ∫_a^b √(1 + [f′(x)]²) dx. Appears in 2017A explicitly. Add this — it's not a standard integration formula but shows up as a trick question where after substitution the integral simplifies nicely.
Partial fractions reminder
For (px+q)/(x²+bx+c) with irreducible quadratic: split as A·(2x+b)/(x²+bx+c) + B/(x²+bx+c). First part → A·ln|x²+bx+c|. Second part: complete the square → arctan. Add the completing-the-square step explicitly to your sheet.
Leibniz rule for power radius
If radius of Σa_n x^n is R, then radius of Σa_n x^{2n} is √R, and of Σa_n (x−c)^n is still R (just shifted). If a_n → L ≠ 0, then R = 1.
Cesàro / Riemann sum limit
lim_{n→∞} (1/n) Σ_{k=1}^n f(k/n) = ∫_0^1 f(x)dx. Used for limits like lim (1^p + … + n^p)^{1/n} — recognize as Riemann sum and evaluate as integral.
Do NOT add: Lagrange error bound (excluded per instructions), ε-δ definition proofs (excluded), Taylor remainder formulas beyond "error ≤ first omitted term for alternating series."
